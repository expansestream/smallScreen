Thank you for taking a look at jfarmsboulder, a Colorado company founded in the summer of 2016, co-managed by Roz Greene and John Brand. The company was initially created to grow certain Plant Select® varieties of xeric plants that will survive the harsh environments of the Colorado Front Range. The company’s management has been interested in commercial growing for several years, and realized that warming global temperatures will enhance and increase the length of the Colorado growing season.

Several indoor growing medium were explored, including natural and artificial lighting, hydroponic and soil, growing year-round, and partial year growing. The most current iteration includes growing seeds in high-quality potting soil and watering with Denver water from the tap. The hydroponic process produces remarkable initial results, only to be decimated by aphids. Where they come from, and how they get indoors is one of life's greatest mysteries.

Optimal growing conditions include starting seeds immediately following the winter solstice, with germination proceeding through Mother's Day, followed by a hardening-off period to mid-June. The company has expanded to manage outdoor growing spaces in excess of 1/2 acre. Xeric varieties include:

While active in St. John’s Episcopal Cathedral’s Green Team, the co-managers were introduced to Dr. Bonnie J. Clark’s book Finding Solace in the Soil. Dr. Clark, the Chair of The University of Denver’s Anthropology Department, recounts 10 field seasons studying the gardens within the Amache internment camp in eastern Colorado. Dr. Clark’s graduate students excavated and documented the growing spaces tended by Japanese Americans between 1942-1945 at Amache. Her book was read while recuperating from hip surgery. One of Dr. Clark’s takeaways is the idea of Senzai, a term applied to Japanese gardening. Senzai has its roots in the ancient Japanese capital of Kyoto and is used to describe a simple garden space, commonly containing at most 5 favorite species. Simple in stature, it is a place of profound peace. We were struck with the realization that according to Japanese gardening, one does not have to plant an entire expanse. Fields of green are nice, but a small space outside a window, on a shelf, in a corner of the yard, or in containers, will do just fine!
