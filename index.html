In addition to obtaining horticultural knowledge unique to Colorado, jfarmsboulder was interested in CSU’s Certified Gardener program because there was a section devoted to lawn care. As new homeowners, jfarms was excited to improve the landscape, transform their yard into a xeric paradise, reduce their water bill; in short, letting the lawn grow in accordance with nature.

One of the first attempts was to transform portions of the front yard into a field of thyme. It never worked because the neighborhood rabbit population made short work of the plantings, even when they were protected with metal-mesh window screen material. Then came the bind weed. It was pervasive. The owners tried to control it by extracting it from its roots, but alias. The tap root of a bind weed can be over 6" long! At this point, it became clear that a “mommy makeover” was in order.

Fortunately, CSU has a publication outlining the steps to redo one’s lawn click here. CSU’s technique works, with the following caveats:

  • The technique recommends using Roundup® as one of the first steps to kill the existing lawn. Roundup® does knock out what was previously planted. It does not, however, inhibit the new growth of the replacement lawn.
  • Block off the lawn into 6"x6" sections that can be easily managed. If one chooses to do an entire yard at once, be prepared to follow through in short order. Unfortunately, jfarms tired doing a large their back section of yard, applying Roundup®, prepping the soil, but delayed planting new seed for 2 weeks. BIG MISTAKE! With nothing in the ground to germinate, the weeds came back with a vengeance!

The seeds of jfarmsboulder were sewn in the fall of 2016. As the nation was gripped in the presidential election, Denver tv channels began reporting mysterious sightings on their weather radar systems. For close to two weeks, light blue images, thought to be fog-like moisture, could be seen on their websites. Public interest soared. Explanations were few. Ground clutter was thought to be the culprit, but no. The images were plumes of Painted Lady butterflies, so dense, that they were detected by radar! When jfarms approached one of their soon-to-be clients, the expectation was that a butterfly garden would induce the Painted Ladies to return year-after-year.

Well, that never happened. What did happen was that a large butterfly garden took shape, and the interest shifted to attracting Monarch Butterflies. In fact, the butterfly garden is registered as a Monarch Way Station, one of the largest in Colorado.

So, where are the butterflies? In the eight years since 2016, there have been butterflies in the garden, primarily Painted Ladies, Swallow Tails, and an occasional Monarch or two. The University of Kansas, the prompter of the Monarch Way Station program, has been monitoring the Monarch population in the United States and Mexico for several years. This is important because like most of us, jfarms loves to see the concluding segment if a David Attenborough or Morgan Freeman nature documentary conclude with massive swarms of Monarch Butterflies crowding the forests of southern Mexico where they spend the winter.

The Monarchs are in trouble. The 2023-2024 over-wintering population in Mexico is the 2nd lowest ever recorded. This is well documented in KU’s Monarch Watch blog click here. Of particular interest is the map of North America showing the flyways of the Monarch. Colorado is on the extreme (the very extreme) western edge of their flight path. Hopefully butterfly gardens like the one managed by jfarmsboulder will entice them to take a second look and spend a while as they pass close to Colorado!

While active in St. John’s Episcopal Cathedral’s Green Team, the co-managers were introduced to Dr. Bonnie J. Clark’s book Finding Solace in the Soil. Dr. Clark, the Chair of The University of Denver’s Anthropology Department, recounts 10 field seasons studying the gardens within the Amache internment camp in eastern Colorado. Dr. Clark’s graduate students excavated and documented the growing spaces tended by Japanese Americans between 1942-1945 at Amache. Her book was read while recuperating from hip surgery. It was an immense source of vision and encouragement, as seen in this self-running PowerPoint:

One of Dr. Clark’s takeaways is the idea of Senzai, a term applied to Japanese gardening. Senzai has its roots in the ancient Japanese capital of Kyoto and is used to describe a simple garden space, commonly containing at most 5 favorite species. Simple in stature, it is a place of profound peace. We were struck with the realization that according to Japanese gardening, one does not have to plant an entire expanse. Fields of green are nice, but a small space outside a window, on a shelf, in a corner of the yard, or in containers, will do just fine!
